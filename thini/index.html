01

The Story That Changes Everything

Chef Aon Krittathiranon grew up in Chiang Mai, in the mountainous north of Thailand. He ran Red Chili Cooking School there, a top-rated culinary school teaching the regional traditions of Northern Thai cuisine to students who came from around the world to learn them.

In 2019, Matt and Jason Adelman, the Oberlin brothers behind The Feve, traveled to Chiang Mai. Through a chain of friends and coincidence they met Aon as a guide. He invited them home and cooked for them. They recognized immediately what they were tasting.

They asked him to move to Oberlin, Ohio to open a restaurant together. He said yes. He gave Red Chili Cooking School to his brother, packed up his life, and moved 12 time zones away from his family to bring the food of Chiang Mai to a small American college town.

ThìNì means "is here" in Thai. It is not a restaurant name. It is a declaration. Thailand is here. And yet the person Aon loves most is not.

It is now 2026. ThìNì Thai survived a pandemic that arrived two months after opening, built a cult following on carryout orders, earned a 4.7-star rating across nearly 300 reviews, and moved into a bold new building on South Main Street painted in deep aubergine and hot pink with gold trim on the roofline. Aon imports 90 percent of his ingredients from Thailand. He brought the charcoal grill. The dinnerware. The mural on the building says, in Thai: "With love from Thailand."

His wife is still there. A visa situation with no clear resolution has kept them separated for years. He cooks every service, four nights a week, while she waits on the other side of the world.

He said on record: "I work because I love to. That is why I'm happy." That sentence, given what he is carrying, is the most quietly extraordinary thing a chef has ever said.

"My dream and passion is always to show my food to the world. I want people to experience Thailand as it is in the food where I am from."
— Chef Aon Krittathiranon  ·  ThìNì Thai, Oberlin Ohio
02

The Food

ThìNì Thai does not serve generalized Thai food. Aon cooks Northern Thai cuisine, the specific regional traditions of Chiang Mai, dishes that are almost entirely absent from American restaurant culture. Khao Soi, the comfort food of the mountainous north. Gang Hung Ley Moo, slow-stewed pork belly curry with pickled garlic and ginger. Gang Aum Beef fortified with lemongrass, shrimp paste, and Galangal. Curries made from freshly mortared herbs and spices every single day.

90 percent of ingredients are imported directly from Thailand, including the charcoal for the grill and the restaurant's own dinnerware. The bar uses Thai herb tinctures. Nothing is Americanized. When Matt Adelman brought Aon to Oberlin they made a pact: "He knew he would not tweak recipes to favor an American palette. He's very proud of the culinary uniqueness that the region he is from possesses. It shines through."

"When you take one bite of Thai food, you get all of the flavors: spicy, sour, sweet, salty. One bite, all the flavor." — Chef Aon

Cleveland.com called it "geographic dissociation — a travel experience without the travel." Guests who have traveled extensively in Thailand describe tasting things at ThìNì that they have not found anywhere else in Ohio. Reviewers call it the best Thai food they have ever had, including in Bangkok.

This is not a restaurant that needs to be qualified by its geography. It competes on its own terms.

The 2027 Window

This is not a someday conversation

Michelin has confirmed the inaugural Great Lakes Guide publishes in 2027. Inspectors are actively surveying the region right now. ThìNì Thai is 35 minutes from Cleveland. Inspectors visit anonymously, pay their own bill, and return multiple times. One of them may have already sat down at a table in Oberlin.

This changes the entire architecture of the strategy. The original path was: build press, earn Beard recognition, position for Michelin over several years. That sequencing assumed Michelin was a distant horizon. It is not. The 2027 guide is a fixed deadline, and the inspection window is open right now.

The press strategy, the Beard nominations, the social presence, the culinary community relationships — all of it still matters, and matters urgently. But it now serves two masters simultaneously. It is building national visibility while also building the cultural record that makes Michelin's decision to include ThìNì Thai legible to the world when the guide drops.

There is a difference between press that earns recognition and press that contextualizes recognition that may already be coming. Both are worth pursuing. Both are worth pursuing now.

What the 2027 guide means

The Great Lakes region guide will cover Cleveland and surrounding areas. A restaurant 35 minutes from the city, producing nationally significant cuisine, with five years of documented consistency, is exactly what Michelin looks for when expanding to a new market.

What Beard + Michelin together means

A James Beard semifinalist listing in early 2027 landing simultaneously with Michelin Guide publication is not a coincidence to hope for. It is a target to build toward deliberately. That is the moment that puts ThìNì Thai on the national map permanently.

04

The Michelin Mindset

Understanding how Michelin thinks is as important as understanding what they evaluate. The guide is not a popularity contest, a press competition, or a social media award. It is a judgment made by anonymous inspectors who visit multiple times, pay their own way, and measure experience against an absolute standard.

The five criteria Michelin inspectors evaluate are fixed and have not changed in over a century. ThìNì Thai's standing against each one matters now, not in 2027.

Already there
Quality of ingredients

90% imported directly from Thailand. Freshly mortared herbs and spices daily. Charcoal and dinnerware brought from Chiang Mai. This criterion is met at a level few restaurants anywhere achieve.

Already there
Mastery of technique

Northern Thai regional traditions executed without compromise. Aon trained, ran his own culinary school, and has been refining these dishes for years. The reviews confirm mastery consistently.

Already there
Personality of the chef in the cuisine

No restaurant in Ohio has a more distinct culinary identity. Every dish is an expression of one chef's specific origin, philosophy, and sacrifice. Michelin can taste a point of view. This one is unmistakable.

Already there
Value for the price

Moderate price point for food of this caliber and sourcing. Guests who travel an hour and call it worth every mile are making this case in 290+ reviews without being asked to.

Already there
Consistency across visits

4.7 stars maintained across nearly 300 reviews over five years including a pandemic. This is the criterion most restaurants fail. ThìNì Thai has documented evidence of passing it at scale.

Build and protect
The full dining experience

Michelin scores the entire experience, not just the food. Front of house warmth, hospitality consistency, the moment someone walks in to the moment they leave. This needs to match what comes out of the kitchen every single service.

The most important operational principle

Every table between now and the 2027 guide publication should be treated as if a Michelin inspector is sitting at it. Not in a way that changes anything about how Aon cooks, because what he is already doing is the reason this conversation exists. In the sense that the entire experience, front to back, needs to be as deliberate and consistent as the food. Michelin visits multiple times. The first visit might already have happened. The second might be next week.

There is one thing Michelin requires that no restaurant can manufacture: time. Consistency documented over years, not months. ThìNì Thai has five years of it. That is the rarest credential on the list and it already exists.

05

James Beard: The Parallel Track

The Beard Awards and Michelin are not competing priorities. They are the same priority expressed in two different systems. Both reward the same things: culinary excellence, a distinct point of view, and consistency that holds over time. Pursuing both simultaneously is not overreach. It is the correct strategy.

The Beard Awards nomination window opens September through November 2026. That is the most important near-term deadline on this calendar. A Beard semifinalist announcement in early 2027 landing in the same season as the Michelin Great Lakes Guide publication is not luck. It is sequencing.

Target category

Best Chef: Great Lakes. Covers Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Illinois, Wisconsin, and Minnesota. This is the correct category for Chef Aon Krittathiranon and the right place to build recognition in the nomination record.

How nominations work

Peer-driven. Any food industry professional, writer, or member of the public can nominate at jamesbeard.org. The path to a nomination is about who knows the restaurant exists. Every writer and chef who visits is a potential nominator.

  • Every food writer who visits and loves the restaurant should be asked to consider nominating — explicitly and personally
  • Build relationships with Cleveland-area chefs who can become advocates within the industry nomination community
  • Earned media creates a paper trail nominators reference when making their case to the committee
  • Beard nominations compound year over year — even a semifinalist listing in year one changes what is possible in year two
  • The Beard nomination window closes before the Michelin guide publishes — the sequence is right if the work starts now
Why Beard and Michelin reinforce each other

Michelin inspectors do not use Beard nominations as a signal. They visit based on their own intelligence. But the culinary community relationships, the food writer coverage, and the national press presence that the Beard track builds are exactly the same assets that make a restaurant visible to the people who feed Michelin intelligence. These tracks do not run parallel by coincidence. They share an ecosystem.

The Press Packet

The initial pitch is two paragraphs and a link to the press page. No attachments. When an editor responds with interest, the packet goes out. Here is what it contains and why each piece belongs.

  • One-page press release — The 2027 Michelin hook as the lead, not buried. Aon's story woven in as the explanation for why the food is what it is. His quotes from existing interviews, attributed and verifiable. The credential block: 4.7 stars, 290+ reviews, five years, 90% from Thailand, Northern Thai cuisine specifically.
  • Existing press clips — The Oberlin Review interview (2021) and the Cleveland.com feature (2024) show a documented history, not just a current pitch. These are attached as PDFs or linked.
  • Top photographs — Best available images of the food, the kitchen, Chef Aon, and the exterior. High-resolution. These determine whether a national publication can run the story. They are the most important element in the packet after the press release itself.
  • Chef Aon's biography — Chiang Mai, Red Chili Cooking School, the move to Oberlin, the sourcing philosophy, the visa situation if he has agreed to include it. One page maximum.
  • The press page link — claritycompanionllc.com/thini/press. Gives editors everything they need in one place without cluttering the email.
