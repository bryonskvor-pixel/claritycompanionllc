The Story

12 time zones from home. Still cooking.

Chef Aon Krittathiranon grew up in Chiang Mai, in the mountainous north of Thailand. He ran Red Chili Cooking School there, a top-rated culinary school teaching the regional traditions of Northern Thai cuisine. In 2019, Matt and Jason Adelman, Oberlin brothers who own The Feve, traveled to Chiang Mai and met Aon through a string of coincidence. He invited them home and cooked for them.

They recognized immediately what they were tasting. They asked him to move to Oberlin, Ohio to open a restaurant. He said yes. He gave his cooking school to his brother. He moved 12 time zones away from his family to bring the food of Northern Thailand to a small American college town.

ThìNì means "is here" in Thai. It is not a restaurant name. It is a declaration: Thailand is here. Aon has been making it every service, four nights a week, for more than five years.

His wife is still in Thailand. A visa situation with no clear resolution has kept them separated throughout. He travels back to source ingredients — 90 percent of what the restaurant uses comes directly from Thailand, including the charcoal for the grill and the restaurant's own dinnerware. The bar uses Thai herb tinctures. The mural on the building says, in Thai: "With love from Thailand."

ThìNì Thai survived a pandemic that arrived two months after opening, built its following on carryout orders, moved into a striking new building on South Main Street painted in deep aubergine and hot pink with gold on the roofline, and has held a 4.7-star rating across nearly 300 reviews across five years of service. Guests describe driving an hour and calling it the best Thai food they have ever had, including in Bangkok.

He said on record: "I work because I love to. That is why I'm happy." Given everything he is carrying, that is the most quietly extraordinary thing a chef has ever said.

"My dream and passion is always to show my food to the world. I want people to experience Thailand as it is in the food where I am from."

Chef Aon Krittathiranon

The Food

Northern Thai cuisine. Almost entirely absent from American dining.

ThìNì Thai does not serve generalized Thai food. Aon cooks Northern Thai cuisine, the specific regional traditions of Chiang Mai, dishes that are nearly absent from American restaurant culture. Khao Soi, the layered curry noodle soup that is Chiang Mai's signature comfort food. Gang Hung Ley Moo, slow-stewed pork belly curry with pickled garlic and ginger. Gang Aum Beef fortified with lemongrass and Galangal. Curries made from freshly mortared herbs and spices every single day.

When the Adelmans brought Aon to Oberlin, the agreement was absolute: "He knew he would not tweak recipes to favor an American palette. He's very proud of the culinary uniqueness that the region he is from possesses. It shines through."

"When you take one bite of Thai food, you get all of the flavors: spicy, sour, sweet, salty. One bite, all the flavor."
— Chef Aon Krittathiranon
"When you meet Aon, you just get it. His energy is amazing. ThìNì Thai happened because we met him."

Cleveland.com called it "geographic dissociation — a travel experience without the travel." Guests who have traveled extensively in Thailand describe tasting things at ThìNì that they have not found anywhere else in Ohio.
