The Story

12 time zones from home. Still cooking.

Chef Aon Krittathiranon grew up in Chiang Mai, in the mountainous north of Thailand. He ran Red Chili Cooking School there, a top-rated culinary school teaching the regional traditions of Northern Thai cuisine. In 2019, two brothers from Oberlin — Matt and Jason Adelman, owners of The Feve — traveled to Chiang Mai and met Aon through a chain of coincidences. He invited them home and cooked for them.

They recognized immediately what they were tasting. They asked him to move to Oberlin to open a restaurant together. He said yes. He gave his cooking school to his brother. He moved 12 time zones away from his family to bring the food of Northern Thailand to a small American college town.

ThìNì means "is here" in Thai. It is not a restaurant name. It is a declaration: Thailand is here. Aon has been making that declaration every service, four nights a week, for more than five years.

His wife is still in Thailand. A visa situation with no clear resolution has kept them separated for years. He travels back when he needs to source ingredients — 90 percent of what the restaurant uses comes directly from Thailand, including the charcoal for the grill and the restaurant's own dinnerware. And then he returns to Oberlin and cooks.

The mural on the building says, in Thai: "With love from Thailand."

He built his restaurant through a pandemic, two months after opening, on carryout orders alone. He moved to a bold new building on South Main Street painted in deep aubergine and hot pink with gold trim on the roofline. He has earned a 4.7-star rating across nearly 300 reviews. Guests describe driving an hour and calling it the best Thai food they have ever had — including in Bangkok.

He is still here. She is still waiting.

"My dream and passion is always to show my food to the world. I want people to experience Thailand as it is in the food where I am from."

Chef Aon Krittathiranon

ThìNì Thai, Oberlin, Ohio

The Food

Northern Thai cuisine. Almost entirely absent from American dining.

ThìNì Thai does not serve generalized Thai food. Aon cooks Northern Thai cuisine — the specific regional traditions of Chiang Mai, dishes that are almost entirely absent from American restaurant culture. Khao Soi, the comfort food of the north, made from curry paste, Burmese curry powder, and coconut milk served over wheat noodles with crispy egg noodles on top. Gang Hung Ley Moo, a slow-stewed pork belly curry loaded with pickled garlic and ginger. Gang Aum Beef fortified with lemongrass, shrimp paste, and Galangal.

Curries are made from freshly mortared herbs and spices every day. The bar uses Thai herb tinctures. Nothing is Americanized. Nothing is softened for a different palate. When Matt Adelman brought Aon to Oberlin, they made a pact: "He knew he would not tweak recipes to favor an American palette. He's very proud of the culinary uniqueness that the region he is from possesses."
