01

The Story That Changes Everything

Chef Aon Krittathiranon grew up in Chiang Mai, in the mountainous north of Thailand. He ran a top-rated culinary school there, Red Chili Cooking School, teaching the regional traditions of Northern Thai cuisine to students who came from around the world to learn them.

In 2019, Matt and Jason Adelman — the Oberlin brothers behind The Feve — traveled to Chiang Mai. Through a chain of friends and coincidence, they met Aon as a guide. He invited them back to his home and cooked for them. They recognized immediately that what they were tasting was something rare.

They asked him to move to Oberlin, Ohio to open a restaurant together. He said yes. He gave Red Chili Cooking School to his brother, packed up his life, and moved 12 time zones away from his family to bring the food of Chiang Mai to a small American college town.

ThìNì means "is here" in Thai. It is not a restaurant name. It is a declaration. Thailand is here. And yet the person Aon loves most is not.

It is now 2026. ThìNì Thai has survived a pandemic that arrived two months after opening, built a cult following on carryout orders, earned a 4.7-star rating across nearly 300 reviews, and moved into a bold new building on South Main Street painted in deep aubergine and hot pink with gold trim on the roofline. Aon imports 90 percent of his ingredients from Thailand. He brought the charcoal grill. He brought the dinnerware. The mural on the building says, in Thai: "With love from Thailand."

His wife is still there.

A visa situation with no clear resolution has kept them separated for years. He cooks every service, four nights a week, in Oberlin, Ohio, while she waits on the other side of the world.

"My dream and passion is always to show my food to the world. I want people to experience Thailand as it is in the food where I am from."
— Chef Aon Krittathiranon
02

The Food

This is not a generalized Thai restaurant. ThìNì Thai serves Northern Thai cuisine — the food of Chiang Mai, a regional tradition that is almost entirely absent from American dining. The dishes are rooted in the specific herbs, spices, and techniques of the mountainous north: Khao Soi, Gang Hung Ley Moo, Gang Aum Beef, Boat Noodle Soup. The Penang curry. Dishes that exist here because one chef refused to approximate.

Aon imports 90 percent of ingredients from Thailand, including the charcoal used for grilling and the restaurant's dinnerware. Curries are made from freshly mortared herbs and spices. The bar uses Thai herb tinctures in handcrafted cocktails. Nothing is Americanized. Nothing is softened.

"When you take one bite of Thai food, you get all of the flavors: spicy, sour, sweet, salty. One bite, all the flavor." — Chef Aon

Guests who have traveled extensively in Thailand describe tasting things at ThìNì that they have not found elsewhere in Ohio. The food writer from cleveland.com described it as "geographic dissociation — a travel experience without the travel." Reviewers routinely say it is the best Thai food they have ever had, including in Bangkok.

Matt Adelman, who studied at the Cleveland Institute of Art, designed the space as a canvas. Together, he and Aon have built something that does not ask to be compared to other Thai restaurants in Ohio because it is not competing with them.

03

Why James Beard Comes Before Michelin

Michelin does not currently cover Ohio. Their guide expands based on regional culinary destination worthiness, not individual restaurant quality alone. The path to Michelin runs through national visibility, and national visibility runs through the James Beard Awards.

Beard covers the Midwest actively. The Best Chef: Great Lakes category exists specifically for restaurants like ThìNì Thai. A Beard semifinalist listing creates:

  • →National visibility with food writers, critics, and award committees
  • →Credibility that makes Michelin take notice when Ohio enters the guide
  • →Peer recognition that drives further nominations and coverage
  • →A documented record of excellence national press can reference

With Aon's story now part of the public narrative, the timeline accelerates. This is no longer a restaurant waiting for the right writer to discover it. It is a story waiting to be told by the right writer, on its own terms.

06

The Press Kit

Before outreach begins, the restaurant needs a press kit ready to send on request. The initial pitch email contains no attachments — just the story and a link to the press page. The kit goes out when interest is confirmed.

  • Chef Aon's full biography — Chiang Mai background, Red Chili Cooking School, the move to Oberlin, his sourcing philosophy
  • Matt and Jason Adelman's story — how they met Aon in Thailand and what they built together
  • High-resolution photography — minimum 10 images at 300 DPI: food, kitchen, dining room, Chef Aon, the exterior
  • Menu with descriptions that communicate sourcing and technique behind each dish
  • Key review excerpts and rating summary — 4.7 stars, 290+ reviews, press clips to date
  • A note on Northern Thai cuisine specifically — what distinguishes it from the generalized Thai food most Americans know
  • Reservation and contact information
Photography is the single most important investment

A great photo of Aon in the kitchen — his hands, his wok, his focus — does more work than every word in the kit. Without professional food photography, national publications will not run the story regardless of how compelling the pitch is. A Cleveland-area food photographer typically runs $500–$1,500 for a session. This is the first expenditure to make.

08

James Beard: The Specifics

The Beard nomination process is peer-driven. The path to a nomination is about who knows the restaurant exists. Every writer, chef, and industry professional who walks through that door is a potential nominator.

Nomination timeline

Nominations open each fall, typically September through November, at jamesbeard.org. Anyone can submit. Semifinalists are announced late winter, finalists in spring, winners at the June ceremony.

Target category

Best Chef: Great Lakes. Covers Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Illinois, Wisconsin, and Minnesota. This is the correct category for Chef Aon Krittathiranon.

  • Every food writer who visits and loves the restaurant is a potential nominator — ask them
  • Build relationships with Cleveland-area chefs who can become advocates within the industry
  • Earned media creates a paper trail nominators reference when making their case to the committee
  • The nomination record compounds. Year three matters more than year one. Start now.
09

What Michelin Actually Looks For

Michelin inspectors visit anonymously, pay their own bills, and return multiple times before deciding. They evaluate five criteria. ThìNì Thai already meets all of them.

  • ✓Quality of ingredients — 90% imported directly from Thailand
  • ✓Mastery of technique — Northern Thai regional traditions, freshly mortared daily
  • ✓Personality of the chef in the cuisine — Aon's presence is in every plate
  • ✓Value for the price — moderate price point, exceptional quality
  • ✓Consistency across visits — 4.7 stars across 290+ reviews over years of service
The one thing that cannot be rushed

Ohio is not currently a Michelin market. Getting it there is a regional conversation the entire Cleveland culinary community needs to have together. National media attention for restaurants like ThìNì Thai is the strongest argument for why Ohio deserves a guide. Every piece of press is also a vote for that expansion.

"With love from Thailand" — painted on the building where a man cooks his heart out, four nights a week, waiting for the world to catch up.
